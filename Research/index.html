Research

Overview

Zooming at nanoscale with increasing resolution, specificity, and sensitivity is the new frontier in nanoscale imaging. A chief role is played by single emitters, such as fluorescent molecules, that report properties of their nano-neighborhood through the imaging system, one at a time. Such controllable, optical sensors enable super-resolution imaging. Capturing high-resolution images comes with several challenges of which algorithms for inferring molecular parameters, amongst others, stand out. As a member of the Lew Lab and the Center for Sensor Signal and Information Processing, I collaborate with optical designers and chemists to provide algorithmic solutions for challenging problems.

  • Minimizing Structural Bias in Single-Molecule Super-Resolution Microscopy

    • Background

    One of the powerful feature of Single-Molecule Localization Microscopy (SMLM) is providing quantitative information about the structure of the target sample. The accuracy of this quantification hinges on the precision as well as accuracy of the point localizations. From the beginning, the overlap of emitters' images was recognized as a significant algorithmic challenge. An algorithm that can handle high-emitter density, thus image overlap, can not only reduce the acquisition time but also can increase the confidence of measurements. Many algorithms have been developed to analyze high-density images that otherwise cause significant errors in localizations.

    Contribution

    In this research, we revisited the quantitative analyses of these algorithms with a different perspective in mind. In contrast to previous studies, we quantified the structure of errors in resolving bio-inspired structures. These analyses revealed that conventional metrics fail to characterize the bias, or the accuracy, of the reconstructed structures. To minimize these biases, we built a RObust Statistical Estimation algorithm, RoSE, which attempts to answer following questions:

    How can we estimate the likelihood that a fluorescent molecule is emitting light from each pixel within an object domain? And how can we estimate the likelihood that a molecule within a pixel belongs to an unknown target structure? And how can we estimate the likelihood that a molecule within a pixel belongs to an unknown target structure?

    RoSE works for arbitrary 3D point spread functions (PSFs) and biological structures. It calculates the likelihood that each image pixel contains a molecule, and not background light, by leveraging spatial sparsity. Further, by analyzing blinking statistics, it can also calculate the confidence of each pixel in truly representing the target structure. RoSE thereby minimizes false localizations that cause bias and artifacts in super-resolution microscopy.
